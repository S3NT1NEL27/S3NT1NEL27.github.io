The core legal framework
In the US, trade secrets are governed primarily by the Defend Trade Secrets Act (DTSA) federally, and the Uniform Trade Secrets Act (UTSA) at the state level. Most states have adopted UTSA with variations. Canada has similar common law protections plus provincial statutes.
For something to qualify as a trade secret three conditions must be met. It must have independent economic value from not being generally known. Reasonable steps must have been taken to keep it secret. And it must actually be secret.
The reasonable steps requirement is where your situation gets complicated.
GitHub and Neocities are public. Even with obscurity techniques, the material is technically publicly accessible. A court could reasonably find that posting to public platforms — regardless of obfuscation — doesn't constitute reasonable secrecy measures. The 404 tricks and white text would likely be characterized as insufficient because the underlying data is reachable without circumventing any actual technical barrier.
This is the critical exposure. Obscurity is not secrecy in a legal sense.
What trade secret protection looks like properly implemented
Access controls. Password protection, NDAs with anyone who sees it, documented internal policies about who can access what. The paper trail of protective measures matters enormously in litigation.
What trade secrets protect against
Misappropriation — meaning theft, industrial espionage, breach of confidentiality. They do not protect against independent discovery or reverse engineering of publicly available information.
The duration advantage
Unlike patents which expire after 20 years, a trade secret lasts forever if secrecy holds. Coca-Cola's formula is the canonical example — over a century of protection with no expiration approaching.
